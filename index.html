Field notes · El Paso County, TX · FIPS 48141

Where the jobs land,
where the workers live.

Between 2011 and 2023, El Paso County's job map shifted. This survey lays two federal labor datasets side by side — LEHD LODES commuting flows and QWI job creation records — to trace where new employment took root, and where the people who fill those jobs come home to at night.

BM · 01 — the overview

Two different questions, both worth asking.

"Where are the jobs" and "where do the workers live" sound like the same question, but LODES answers them from two separate files, and they tell different stories. A tract can gain jobs without gaining residents, and a neighborhood can send thousands more commuters into the workforce without a single new employer opening inside it. Both halves of that story are below, and the rest of this page keeps them separate on purpose.

BM · 05 — job creation vs. destruction

New jobs, and the ones that disappeared.

Everything so far compares two snapshots, 2011 and 2023 — it can't tell you whether the path between them was steady growth or a rollercoaster. LODES alone also can't tell a new job from an old one moving addresses. The Quarterly Workforce Indicators can do both: this is the county's annual job creation and destruction, drawn from a longitudinal work history rather than a single snapshot.

Sources: U.S. Census Bureau LEHD Origin-Destination Employment Statistics (LODES v8) and Quarterly Workforce Indicators (QWI). Planning district boundaries: City of El Paso Plan Areas. LODES counts show worker home/workplace pairs at a point in time and do not by themselves distinguish new job creation from relocation of existing jobs; QWI job creation/destruction figures are the more reliable measure of new employment. Map colors are classed by quantile, not a continuous scale, so shade reflects which bracket a tract falls into rather than its exact value — use the legend ranges or click a tract for the number.
